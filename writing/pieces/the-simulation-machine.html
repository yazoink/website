1. The Foundations


How accurately can you artificially simulate life?


Imagine a computer with all the knowledge in the universe -- everything that has ever happened. It could then, throetically, predict the future -- everything that ever will happen -- through cause-and-effect. This is probably impossible.


We can explain a lot of the mechanisms of reality through the medium of science. We can predict the weather with decent accuracy, and we know that the Earth revolves around the Sun. We know that water will boil at 100 degrees and freeze at 0. We can disassemble, analyse, and deconstruct our physical reality to a fair extent. We can also somewhat predict what other people are thinking and feeling through signifiers such as language and behavior.


We are given the impression that reality has some sort of a solid foundation -- a concrete foundational web of cause-and-effect -- quantifiable rules and concrete definitions. But, obviously, we cannot explain everything yet.


Science can tell us that the speed of light is 299,792,458 meters per second, and that nothing can exceed it. It's a solid datum -- holds a cool, reassuring air. It tells us of a precise point on which we can comfortably stand in the physical world. Good to know.


But if we are to regard this as immutable scientific truth, how do we, then, factor in black holes? It's an ineluctable contradiction. The gravity of a black hole traps everything, including light. A Black hole requires the impossible -- none of our comfortable worldly datums can apply in its presence.


There are circumstances in which no known laws of science can apply. I am not saying that it will never be possible for science to explain these phenomena, but that any of our "fixed" rules can broken at any time. Ever seen a ghost?...


But the universe will die one day -- at least as we know it. Nothing is invincible and nothing is permanent. Everything will give at some point -- including the laws of nature. Regardless, as humans, we still classify and enumerate some seemingly solid laws of reality. We deconstruct it down to the raw materials of billion-year-old carbon (we are stardust, we are golden...). But exactly how far can we deconstruct this physical reality?


In The Myth of Sisyphus, Albert Camus says:


In psychology as in logic, there are truths but no truth. Socrates' "Know thyself" has as much value as the "Be virtuous" of our confessionals. They reveal a nostalgia at the same time as an ignorance. They are sterile exercises on great subjects. They are legitimate only in precisely so far as they are approximate.


And here are trees and I know their gnarled surface, water and I feel its taste. These scents of grass and stars at night, certain evenings when the heart relaxes -- how shall I negate this world whose power and strength I feel? Yet all the knowledge on earth will give me nothing to assure me that this world is mine. You describe it to me and you teach me to classify it. You enumerate its laws and in my thirst for knowledge I admit that they are true. You take apart its mechanism and my hope increases. At the final stage you teach me that this wondrous and multicolored universe can be reduced to the atom and that the atom itself can be reduced to the electron. All this is good and I wait for you to continue. But you tell me of an invisible planetary system in which electrons gravitate around a nucleus. You explain this world to me with an image. I realize then that you have been reduced to poetry: I shall never know. Have I the time to become indignant? You have already changed theories. So that science that was to teach me everything ends up in a hypothesis, that lucidity founders in metaphor, that uncertainty is resolved in a work of art.


We can dissect our world down to certain building blocks, but if we keep going, we hit a wall where all knowledge ends. We have no foundational knowledge of anything. How can we, then, claim anything as concrete scientific truth?


We don't understand the system; we are simply making approximations within the confines its walls. Scientists will say that the universe started with "The Big Bang" but nobody can actually define what "The Big Bang" means; it all just boils down to semantic padding around the eternal question mark. All of human understanding is construction; a precarious sand-castle -- subject to collapse under too much or the wrong kind of pressure.


Is it possible to measure or enumerate anything in this reality; can anything truly be placed in a concrete categorical box? Or is it all so complex and infinite that there cannot be more than one singular instance of any given thing?... Space stretches out infinitely in all directions. Must it, then, be infinitely divisible? There is no limit to the amount of times a number can be divided, just as there is no limit to the number of times it can be multiplied.


2. The Truth


In a world of infinity, nothing can be solidly defined; including "truth." You can't touch "truth." Everyone who uses the word is referring to something different.


The words "fact" and "science" are often imbued with an undue heaviness. -- If your reaction to this statement was a flash-bulb of suspicion, you are further demonstrating my point. They appear as if they expand beyond the human scope to something celestial and untouchable. But you must remember that words serve solely as abstractions -- sets of common traits and associations dressed up in vaudeville costumes; outlandish, absurd, and obscene. They are actors. They are an elaborate system for encoding the abstract material of thought into a standardised, digestible format.


Words operate in the same man-made binary realm as the computer and the facts of science, and by their very nature, can never be universally defined because it is impossible to wholly communicate anything in the infinite realm. The encoding is lossy at best. Take this passage from The Doors of Perception by Aldous Huxley:


We live together, we act on, and react to, one another; but always and in all circumstances we are by ourselves. The martyrs go hand in hand into the arena; they are crucified alone. Embraced, the lovers desperately try to fuse their insulated ecstasies into a single self-transcendence; in vain. By its very nature every embodied spirit is doomed to suffer and enjoy in solitude. Sensations, feelings, insights, fancies -- all these are private and, except through symbols and at second hand, incommunicable. We can pool information about experiences, but never the experiences themselves. From family to nation, every human group is a society of island universes.


Definitions depend on Whole communication -- full seamless sender-to-reciever operation. How else are we supposed to come to an agreement on anything? Whole communication is impossible within the human scope, as every living thought is built from the raw materials of circumstance and memory.


The meaning of a word is primarily circumstantial -- it's vague and blurry on its own. A word of which you do not know the meaning takes on the meaning of the absence of its meaning. Every word has different connotations to each individual person, under every individual circumstance. Most of the meaning we derive from words is inferred from context: if you have a memory associated with a certain word, your definition will always be tainted with it. The same word can mean something completely different when placed in a different context. A sentence can take on an entirely different meaning with the addition or removal or manipulation of a singular word. Every time a word enters your consciousness a new connotation is annexed. The factors sprawl out as infinite as space.


Denotation is a myth. Definitions drift, mutate, metastasize, and distort. It's the deep archaic desire of the pattern-seeking mind for everything to be contained and secure in a neat box. It's all too common to fall into the trap of viewing the world primarily through these man-made systems. When you sever your perception at these arbitrary definitions and stop-points, your capacity for capturing the information through which you interpret the world is severely limited. It serves to construct a sense of reality that is comfortable, but could collapse at any point.


To convey a Whole thought would be to convey the Whole universe. Where is the line at which a thought ends? There is no line. Whole communication can only exist in a finite binary realm.


Nonetheless, we define and communicate everything, including our "laws of reality" through this neutered, malleable medium of words; and it's not difficult to see why humanity has such an affinity for it: aside from the obvious point of its convenience, we know that it serves as a system of abstractions -- approximations and enumerations of patterns. We are wired to be pattern-seekers. Where would we be without this behavior? We are drawn to it because it lets us put things in neat boxes. We have a box for danger, a box for safety, a box for food, a box for family, a box for friends, a box for enemies... the list goes on until you have a whole vocabulary of patterns.


The conscious condition is to match patterns in a realm of infinity.


3. The Machine, Real and in the Flesh


The prediction machine I mentioned earlier bears a lot of similarities to Artificial Intelligence. It may even serve as a logical conclusion to it.


There has been a rising trend of attempts at training Large Language Models to emulate human beings (commonly seen on platforms such as character.ai) and there is a large ongoing debate as to whether AI could ever gain sentience (or perhaps it already has?).


The base mechanism of Artificial Intelligence is an algorithm that teaches itself. LLMs are fed large sets of data which get spliced and reconfigured in optimal accordance to a prompt. There is some similarity between this and the mechanism of human memory and pattern recognition. How much data must be fed to an AI before it can accurately simulate a human being?


On the 14th of September, 2025, a megachurch in Texas played an AI-generated audio clip of Charlie Kirk saying words he never said to a congregation who gave it a standing ovation. This reaction, along with the popularity of character.ai and similar apps, suggests that it doesn't need much in order to at least be somewhat convincing.


Will AI ever be capable of accurately emulating a human reaction? Exactly what somebody would say, given a certain prompt? It amounts to a false comparison because the human being and the computer reside in entirely different realms. An AI is capable of identifying certain themes in the data its given, such as speech patterns or certain biases. It may be able to construct responses that appear to fit your "box" pertaining to a human individual. It may be convincing, but it will forever remain as accurate as a broken clock. The mental model you keep of an individual is an encoding of infinite fragmented memories and associations -- it is an arbitrary, reductive category, just as the Word.


The action or reaction of a human being is founded on an infinite number of factors which are unique to individual memories, neural connections, and circumstances. These factors are constantly in flux and can never be repeated. The machine is confined to a binary realm; its entire world can be reduced to a finite number of bits. All communication from the infinite realm must be filtered and encoded accordingly -- ...data lost and garbled in translation -- diffused accuracy of a dream; twisted spectral face of a memory...


Which version are you trying to emulate? The human condition is not stable. Blood is pumping, stomach digesting, cells dying, regenerating, sleep is on the horizon, headache burgeoning, black mold on the ceiling -- an effluvium of infinity radiating from every orifice -- old Mortein jingle smeared across the door in ectoplasm... -- Which precise moment of living human time? Can you pick out a singular point? Divide it into units? A singular moment -- take a snapshot? I certainly can't. Your time is incompatible with mine, you understand? Beyond your scope.


The condition of computer is wholly stable. It exists in its own isolated universe, completely incompatible with the Living; at your mercy. The human essence is out of scope. The living world cannot be translated into its language. The raw materials of life cannot be captured or simulated.


The computer can never achieve anything beyond finite pattern matching and cause-and-effect. It is perpetually bound to true and false because they are the fundamental building blocks of its world.


Nothing is true. Everything is permitted.
