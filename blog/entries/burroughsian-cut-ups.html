Recently, I have found an interest in the "cut-up" technique of writing pioneered by William S. Burroughs, with the influence of Brion Gysin, in the 1950s and 1960s. This technique involves the cutting and rearranging of text, transmuting it into a new body in order to establish new meanings and connections.


The primary inspiration behind the cut-up was the collage technique often employed in visual arts. Its purpose is to create a text that is closer to memory and perception than traditional representational writing and art. It should be noted that the results of this technique are often reminiscent of dream speech, psychotic speech, hypnogogia, and abstract thought processes.


Burroughs states in The Adding Machine:


I have explained how in 1959 Brion Gysin said that writing is fifty years behind painting and applied the montage technique to writing — a technique which had been used in painting for fifty years. As you know, painters had the whole representational position knocked out from under them by photography, and there was in fact a photography exhibition around the turn of the century entitled ‘Photography — The Death of Painting’. Premature, but painting did have to get a new look. So painters turned first to montage.

Now the montage is actually much closer to the facts of perception, than representational painting. Take a walk down a city street and put what you have just seen down on canvas. You have seen half a person cut in two by a car, bits and pieces of street signs and advertisements, reflections from shop windows — a montage of fragments. And the same thing happens with words. Remember that the written word is an image. Brion Gysin’s cut-up method consists of cutting up pages of text and re-arranging them in montage combinations.


I decided to attempt to create a piece of writing using cut-ups, drawing the sources from a collection of quotes from books that I’ve been building recently; hand-writing them on scraps of paper in order to commit them to my memory. I have been trying to extract connections, common themes, philosophies, motifs, from these quotes in order to understand them more and solidify some kind of philosophy or world-view to live by (this could be a whole tangent in itself but I’ll just leave it here and potentially write about it in detail another time). The idea was that using cut-ups could loosen my view of these books and quotes and reduce them to loose concepts, ideas, and imagery; ready to run along the veins of free association.

Burroughs, Allen Ginsberg, Albert Camus, Jean-Paul Sartre, Aldous Huxley, Richard Farina, Gilles Deleuze, Jack Kerouac, and one each from Hunter S. Thompson and Sylvia Plath.
